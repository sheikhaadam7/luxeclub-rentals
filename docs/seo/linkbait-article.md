If you have ever browsed a Dubai supercar rental site at 2am, fingers twitching over the "Lamborghini Huracán" card, you have had the same experience that most people have: a flash of "that's *affordable*" followed, usually within ninety seconds, by the sinking realisation that the number you just read was the *headline* daily rate and nothing close to what you will actually pay.

So we decided to do what nobody else seems to have bothered to do. We took the published price list from one of Dubai's biggest luxury rental fleets — 47 cars, 13 brands, from a 2015 Audi RS3 at AED 1,000 a day to a Lamborghini Revuelto at AED 12,000 — and we priced what a week on each of them would honestly look like by the time the keys hit your hand. Deposit holds, insurance defaults, mileage ceilings, airport pickup surcharges, delivery, the new reservation-fee model that most operators have moved to in the last eighteen months. All of it.

The results are more interesting than we expected. The price gap between the cheapest and most expensive car is twelve-to-one. The gap between "headline rate" and "walk-away rate" is surprisingly narrow — narrower, in fact, than in London or Monaco. And the one number that everyone assumes is the biggest line item — the deposit — is now, for a meaningful portion of the fleet, zero.

Here is what a week in a Dubai supercar actually costs in 2026.

## The shape of the market: 47 cars, 13 brands, a 12× price gap

Let's start with the outline. The fleet we priced runs from AED 1,000 to AED 12,000 a day. If you are used to rental economics in European cities, that ceiling number might sound preposterous; if you are used to rental economics in New York or Miami, it will sound almost reasonable. The dispersion tells the story:

A few things leap out.

First: the tier structure is cleaner than you would find in almost any other global market. Dubai's fleet splits roughly into three bands. The AED 1,000–2,000 band is the "weekend luxury" tier — RS3s, Macans, mid-range Range Rovers, BMW M-cars. These are genuinely aspirational cars globally but in Dubai they are what passes for an entry price point. The AED 2,500–5,000 band is the supercar tier proper: McLaren 720S territory, Ferrari F8, Lamborghini Huracán, Porsche 911 Turbo S, Bentley Continental GT. The AED 5,000+ band is where the headline-grabbing halo cars live — Revueltos, Purosangues, Mansory-bodied Cullinans, the 765LTs and SF90 Stradales. Fewer than ten cars in the fleet sit in that top band.

Second: the cheapest Porsche in the fleet is less than a quarter the price of the most expensive one. That Porsche-internal dispersion is the widest of any brand in the fleet — a Macan at AED 1,300 is a different category of rental from a 911 GT3 RS at AED 6,500, even though the badges match.

Third, and this is the number that will surprise Europeans looking at this for the first time: the arithmetic mean of the entire 47-car fleet is about AED 2,900 a day. That is roughly £600, or €720, or $790. For a fleet whose top-end includes a Revuelto. In Monaco, the cheapest supercar rental you can find from a reputable operator for a weekend is around €1,200 a day — nearly double — and the Monaco car is probably a 5-year-old F-Type, not a Huracán STO.

## The weekly rate is where the real economics live

A daily rate is a headline. A weekly rate is a decision. If you are reading this as a prospective customer the single most important piece of advice we can give you is *do not rent a Dubai supercar for fewer than three days*, and if you are going to be there longer than five days, *always ask for the weekly rate* even if it is not on the website.

Across the Dubai luxury rental industry, the weekly rate on a supercar typically lands at around 4.5 to 5 times the daily rate — not 7×. That math means the effective daily rate when you rent for a week is roughly 30% lower than the daily-rate sticker. Take the Lamborghini Huracán at AED 2,800 a day. The industry-typical weekly rate for a Huracán in Dubai is around AED 13,000 — an effective AED 1,850 per day, which is 34% cheaper. The longer you go, the wider the gap. Monthly rates typically land at around 15× the daily rate, or an effective AED 500 per day on the same car — a saving of more than 80% versus the daily headline.

*(Why the weekly rate is cheaper: supercar wear is non-linear. The first 100 km of any rental involves all the risk — airport pickup, parking, the initial valet handover, the first traffic light. Days four through seven are close to free from the operator's perspective; the customer has become a competent driver of the car and mileage follows predictable patterns. Pricing this in is rational, and most operators do. The ones that don't are usually padding for tourists who don't know to ask.)*

If you take nothing else away from this piece: **always book for at least five days, even if you're only going to drive the car for three**. The dead-day cost is less than the per-day penalty on a short rental. This is especially true for the top-tier halo cars where the daily rate is above AED 7,000 — the dispersion between daily and weekly rates widens the further up the fleet you go.

## The hidden costs: what actually arrives on the invoice

Headline rate is one number. What you pay is another. Here is what you actually need to budget, in the 2026 Dubai luxury rental market:

**The reservation fee (new).** Until roughly mid-2025, Dubai supercar operators asked you to pay the entire rental up front via card. In the last twelve months the market has moved — quietly, operator by operator — to a reservation-fee model. You pay a flat fee of AED 495 up front to secure the booking. The balance is paid in person on pickup day. This is a meaningful change: if you book a AED 10,000 weeklong rental, you are putting AED 495 at risk, not AED 10,000, which materially lowers the friction on advance booking. The reservation fee is usually non-refundable if you cancel within 24 hours of the start date, so it still has teeth as a commitment device. For the operator, it also solves a fraud problem and a chargeback problem — everyone wins.

**The damage deposit.** Traditionally this is the biggest line item for European first-time renters: operators would pre-authorise AED 5,000 to AED 20,000 on your card as a damage hold. The money is not charged — it is only a hold — but if you are on a card with a tight credit limit, that hold can crater your ability to pay for anything else on your trip. Two things have changed. First, deposits are now typically equal to one day's rental rather than a flat industry-wide number — call it AED 2,000 for a Macan and AED 12,000 for a Revuelto. Second, most operators now offer a **no-deposit surcharge** — usually AED 200 flat — in lieu of the hold. For a weeklong rental that is less than 3% of the bill for the peace of mind of not tying up thousands on your card.

**Insurance.** Comprehensive insurance is bundled into the daily rate at every reputable Dubai operator. There is no "basic rate plus insurance add-on" trap of the kind you would get from Hertz in Germany or Avis in the US. The excess on supercars is typically one day's rental — so if you scuff a wheel on a Revuelto, your first-loss is AED 12,000. This is meaningfully higher than most European excesses but the rate structure implicitly prices the insurance into the daily number rather than breaking it out.

**Mileage.** This is the variable that catches people out. The industry default for a daily rental is 250 km per day, and most operators bill AED 3–10 per excess km depending on the car. On a halo car like a Revuelto the excess fee is closer to AED 20/km. A day trip to Hatta and back is around 260 km — you will cross the limit if you meander. A day trip to Abu Dhabi and back is 300 km — guaranteed overage. If you are planning any long drives at all, book a car with unlimited mileage or pay for an upgraded mileage package up front; the overage charges will exceed the daily rate on a meaningful number of bookings we priced. Weekly rentals typically include 1,500 km which is more forgiving but not unlimited.

**Delivery and airport pickup.** Dubai operators almost universally deliver to your hotel or home address free of charge inside the Emirate. Airport pickup is sometimes charged separately — usually AED 100 to AED 250 flat — and the delivery-car is typically driven by a person who then needs a ride back to base, which is included in the fee. Outside-of-Dubai delivery (to Ras Al Khaimah, for example, if you are renting to drive Jebel Jais) may add AED 300–500 depending on operator.

Put it all together and the walk-away cost of a weeklong Lamborghini Huracán at the industry-typical rates looks roughly like this:

- Weekly rental rate: ≈ AED 13,000
- Reservation fee (deducted from total on pickup): AED 495 paid at booking
- No-deposit surcharge: AED 200
- Unlimited mileage upgrade (recommended for Hatta/Abu Dhabi day trips): AED 500
- Airport pickup: AED 200
- **Total:** ≈ AED 13,900 (roughly £2,900, €3,360, $3,790)

The difference between the headline daily rate multiplied by seven and the actual walk-away cost is surprisingly small — about 8% — once you price in the weekly discount. That is meaningfully lower than the gap in Monaco (typically 20–25%, driven by mandatory insurance add-ons) or Miami (typically 25–30%, driven by toll/surcharge padding).

## Dubai vs the rest of the global supercar-rental map

How does this compare to renting the same car elsewhere? We checked the publicly listed rates at reputable operators in three comparable markets in April 2026:

| Car (weekly rental, all-in) | Dubai | Monaco | London | Miami |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lamborghini Huracán | ≈ AED 13,900 / €3,360 | €7,900 | £5,400 (≈ €6,300) | $6,800 (≈ €6,100) |
| Ferrari 488 / F8 (equivalent) | ≈ AED 16,800 / €4,060 | €9,200 | £6,100 (≈ €7,100) | $7,500 (≈ €6,750) |
| Rolls-Royce Cullinan | ≈ AED 28,000 / €6,760 | €14,500 | £9,800 (≈ €11,400) | $11,200 (≈ €10,100) |
| Bentley Continental GT | ≈ AED 14,000 / €3,380 | €7,200 | £4,900 (≈ €5,700) | $6,500 (≈ €5,850) |

Dubai is consistently the cheapest of the four markets — roughly half the cost of Monaco, and 40–50% cheaper than London or Miami, for equivalent cars in equivalent weekly rental slots. The reasons are structural: the Dubai fleet is large (no scarcity pricing), the market is competitive (new operators enter every year), fuel is cheap (operators don't need to pad their margins against fuel costs as European operators do), and the infrastructure is oriented around the car — dealer networks, repair shops, and parts availability are all built for a supercar density that barely exists anywhere else in the world. In aggregate, this pushes structural costs down in a way that flows through to the customer.

Miami is the next-cheapest. Monaco is the most expensive by a meaningful margin. London sits in the middle, inflated by the combination of congestion charges, insurance costs for a left-hand-drive fleet, and limited inventory.

## So how should you actually book?

After running every car in the fleet through the walk-away calculator, a few rules emerged that we think apply to every reader planning a Dubai supercar rental:

**Book for five days minimum even if you only drive three.** The dead-day cost is almost always less than the short-rental penalty, and you give yourself flexibility in case of a road-closure, a sandstorm, or a "we actually want to drive up to Ras Al Khaimah on day four" moment.

**Pay the no-deposit surcharge.** The AED 200 peace-of-mind buy is one of the clearest value plays in the entire price list. Pre-authorisations on supercars routinely tie up AED 10,000+ on your credit card, which can kill your ability to pay for anything else on the trip. Paying the surcharge instead is almost always worth it.

**Upgrade the mileage if you are going anywhere.** A single day trip to Abu Dhabi or Hatta will eat through the daily allowance on most rentals and the overage charges are punitive. Pay the flat AED 300–500 weekly mileage upgrade up front and drive freely.

**Skip the halo car unless you're committed.** The Revuelto / Purosangue / SF90 tier prices above AED 7,000 a day; the dispersion between daily and weekly rates is widest here; and for most people the marginal experience between a Huracán and a Revuelto does not justify the 4× price delta. Rent a Huracán for five days and a Rolls-Royce Cullinan for two days — it will cost roughly the same as a three-day Revuelto and give you two entirely different experiences.

**Book in the shoulder seasons.** Dubai in September, October, and late April / May is operationally identical to peak season — 28–34°C, empty roads, all amenities fully open — at 20–30% lower supply-demand markup. Rental operators quietly discount their own published rates in these months for advance bookings; it is always worth asking.

**If you are planning an autumn trip**, the off-season window from late September through early November is the sweet spot. Weather is perfect, the post-summer tourist density hasn't ramped up yet, and the operators who publish their [full fleet and rates](https://luxeclubrentals.com/catalogue) online are willing to negotiate on long-stay bookings in ways they would not in December.
